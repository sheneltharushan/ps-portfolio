Lecture 01:

Introduction to
Professional Skills

Professional skills are the abilities that help individuals perform effectively in a workplace. They include skills such as communication, teamwork, problem solving, time management, and technical knowledge. These skills improve productivity, support professional growth, and enable employees to handle tasks confidently while maintaining good relationships with colleagues and clients.

Skills necessary for employment

To be employable, a person needs a combination of technical skills, soft skills, and transferable skills that can be used in different roles and industries.

Technical skills

Job specific skills, such as using particular software, operating machines, or applying specialised methods. These are often learned through formal education, training, or hands on experience.

Soft and transferable skills

These skills are valuable in any workplace and can move with a person from one job or industry to another.

  • • Critical thinking and problem solving
  • • Teamwork and collaboration
  • • Time management
  • • Communication skills
  • • Leadership skills

Workplace skills

Skills such as punctuality, reliability, following procedures, and being able to work both under supervision and independently. They reflect how a person behaves and contributes in a professional environment.

Values, beliefs, attitudes, and character

Professional behaviour is influenced not only by skills, but also by the deeper values, beliefs, attitudes, and character that shape how a person responds to challenges and interacts with others.

Values

Fundamental ideas and principles that influence a person's actions and choices. They act as a moral compass and guide how someone responds to challenges and interactions with others.

Beliefs

Personal views about ourselves and the world, shaped by upbringing, cultural background, and experiences. Beliefs influence how we interpret situations and react at work.

Attitudes

The way a person thinks and feels about people, tasks, and situations. A positive and professional attitude supports teamwork, problem solving, and trust in the workplace.

Character

The long term qualities that describe who we are, such as honesty, responsibility, respect, and integrity. Strong character helps others trust and rely on us in a professional setting.

What I learned

Professional abilities like cooperation and communication are essential for career success. Attitudes and values improve mindset and influence behaviour in the workplace. The Johari Window enhances interpersonal relationships and self-awareness. When combined, technical and soft skills improve employability. Everyday interactions provide chances to hone and expand these abilities.

Lecture 02:

CV and Cover
Letter Writing

This lecture focused on how to prepare a clear and professional CV and cover letter. It explained what a CV is, why it matters in job applications, and how careful structure, research, and tailoring can help create a strong first impression with employers.

Understanding CVs

What is a CV?

A document that explains who you are, what you have studied, and the skills and experiences you have. It highlights your education, achievements, abilities, and personal details so employers can judge your suitability.

Purpose of a CV

To show your qualifications and suitability for a specific role. A good CV connects your background to the requirements of the job so the employer can quickly see why you are a strong candidate.

Making a positive first impression

A CV that is clean, easy to read, well organised, and visually appealing is more likely to be noticed. A concise CV that is free of spelling and grammar errors creates a strong professional image.

Writing an effective CV

Tailoring your CV

Customising your CV to each job shows that you have taken time to match your skills to the position. It highlights relevant strengths, such as creativity or technical expertise, that align with the organisation’s needs.

Importance of research

Studying the job description and company information helps you find key requirements and keywords. Using these in your CV improves the chances of passing Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) and matching the position’s expectations.

Structure and formatting

  • • Proper use of headings, bullet points, and white space
  • • Choosing an appropriate font and font size
  • • Maintaining consistent formatting throughout the document

Essential CV components

  • • Personal information
  • • Professional summary
  • • Work experience
  • • Education
  • • Skills
  • • Achievements and awards

Avoiding common mistakes

  • • Spelling and grammar mistakes
  • • Adding information that is not needed or is outdated
  • • Writing too much or too little
  • • Using different styles or formats inconsistently

What I learned

  • • I learned what a CV is and its purpose in job applications.
  • • I understood that a CV showcases skills, qualifications, and experience.
  • • I learned the importance of creating a strong first impression with a clean and well-organised CV.
  • • I realised that tailoring a CV to each job increases the chances of being noticed.

Lecture 03:

Research Writing

What is research writing?

Research writing is a structured way of collecting information, analysing it, and presenting findings in a clear and organised manner. It involves studying a topic, using reliable sources, and writing based on evidence rather than personal opinions. Research writing helps us understand a subject deeply and communicate knowledge accurately.

Purposes of research writing

  • • To explore and understand a specific topic in detail
  • • To find answers to questions or solve problems
  • • To support ideas with facts, data, and evidence
  • • To share new knowledge with others

Research writing includes

  • • Choosing and understanding a research topic
  • • Collecting information from reliable sources
  • • Reviewing existing literature
  • • Analysing data and findings

What I learned

  • • Understanding how to choose and focus on a research topic.
  • • Formulating clear and concise research questions.
  • • Conducting literature reviews and identifying credible sources.
  • • Analysing and interpreting data effectively.
  • • Organising ideas logically in a structured format.

Lecture 04:

Telephone Etiquettes

What are telephone etiquettes?

Telephone etiquette means being polite and professional when talking on the phone. It helps create a good first impression and makes the conversation clear and friendly by following a few simple rules.

Rules for telephone etiquette

  • • Talk clearly and do not eat or chew during the call.
  • • Do not cover the mic so your voice stays clear.
  • • If you need to pause, ask the person to wait and press the hold button.
  • • Be polite and answer the phone quickly (within 2–3 rings).
  • • Use a proper greeting and speak with respect.

Screening calls

  • • Do not ignore or block calls too often, so you keep a good professional image.
  • • Be careful with your tone so you do not upset or offend the caller.

What I learned

  • • Answer calls quickly with a clear and polite greeting.
  • • Ask for permission before placing someone on hold.
  • • Plan calls and prepare key points so you avoid calling again for the same information.
  • • Speak in a friendly and professional way to make each call a positive experience.

Lecture 05:

Email Writing
Etiquettes

What is etiquette?

“Etiquette” means the rules for good and proper behaviour in different situations. In work emails it is important to be clear, polite, and professional so that the message is understood and the tone stays respectful.

Basic rules for email writing

Basic rules

  • • Use correct grammar and spelling.
  • • Do not write in all capitals.
  • • Think before sending and read the email again.
  • • Do not forward unnecessary emails.
  • • Keep messages brief and on point.
  • • Use a clear, meaningful subject line.

What I learned

  • • I understand what emails are for and why we use them in a professional setting.
  • • I learned to check emails carefully before sending to avoid mistakes.
  • • I became more confident writing short, clear, and polite emails.
